# Evaluating Social Reasoning in LLM NPCs through a Controlled Werewolf Game Harness

**An Exploratory Study of Interdisciplinary Scaffolding under Output-Quality Control**

Korean title: **통제된 늑대인간 게임 하네스를 통한 LLM NPC 사회추론 평가**  
Korean subtitle: **출력 품질 통제 하에서의 학제적 스캐폴딩 탐색 연구**

Draft date: 2026-06-29  
Status: public draft, exploratory result

## Abstract

LLM-driven NPCs can produce fluent social dialogue, but fluency alone does not show whether an agent can reason under hidden roles, deception, public pressure, and collective voting. This paper draft presents a controlled 7-player Werewolf game harness for evaluating observable social-reasoning behavior in LLM NPCs. The harness separates raw game-performance metrics from display-level output hygiene, then compares a baseline architecture against a village-side interdisciplinary scaffold.

The scaffold is designed around four interpretable axes: utterance analysis, public-opinion analysis, beneficiary analysis, and interrogation strategy. In the final STEP7-M RT2.3 N=20 comparison, the scaffold arm preserved raw output-quality pass 20/20 and replacements 0 while showing positive but small deltas in village win rate (+0.100), role inference (+0.066), vote consistency (+0.019), and average game duration (+0.150). The role-inference delta did not reach the pre-defined +0.10 strong-claim gate. Therefore, the result is best interpreted as an exploratory positive trend under output-quality control, not as statistically established improvement or general social-reasoning superiority.

## Keywords

LLM NPC, social reasoning, Werewolf, Mafia game, incomplete information, agent evaluation, scaffolding, output-quality control

## 1. Introduction

NPC dialogue generated by large language models can appear socially rich even when the underlying decision process is weak. In a social deduction game, this gap matters. A player must remember previous statements, detect contradictions, update suspicion under partial information, resist crowd pressure, and decide whether a vote benefits the village or the hidden wolf team. A transcript may look plausible while the agent repeatedly votes against its own evidence or amplifies a false accusation.

This project uses a 7-player Werewolf game as a controlled evaluation environment for LLM NPC social reasoning. Werewolf is useful because it combines hidden roles, asymmetric information, public discussion, deception, and collective voting. These properties turn social dialogue into observable behavioral outcomes: who was suspected, who was voted for, whether the executed player was a wolf, and whether the village ultimately won.

The core research question is:

> Can an interdisciplinary scaffold based on utterance analysis, public-opinion tracking, beneficiary analysis, and interrogation strategy change observable social-reasoning behavior in LLM NPCs under a fixed Werewolf game harness?

The answer in the current draft is deliberately conservative. The final RT2.3 result shows small positive deltas after output-quality control, but not enough evidence for a strong performance claim. The contribution is the controlled evaluation setup, the separation between raw performance and display hygiene, and an interpretable scaffold that can be inspected through logs and replay.

## 2. Background and Motivation

Social deduction games are a compact testbed for social reasoning because they require inference from unreliable public speech. Unlike single-agent puzzle tasks, the agent must respond to other agents whose statements may be incomplete, strategic, or deceptive. This makes the setting relevant to LLM NPC research, game AI, and interactive narrative systems.

The scaffold used here draws from several practical reasoning traditions:

| source area | operational interpretation in this project |
|---|---|
| Humanities-style narrative reading | Track contradiction, responsibility avoidance, and narrative consistency |
| Social psychology | Track conformity pressure, bandwagoning, scapegoating, and public opinion shifts |
| Game theory | Ask who benefits from a vote, silence, accusation, or strategic ambiguity |
| Board-game deduction practice | Use interrogation, delayed judgment, follow-up questions, and role-protection awareness |

The goal is not to claim that these fields are fully modeled. The goal is to convert human-readable social reasoning concepts into a scaffold that produces observable changes in game logs.

## 3. Environment

The environment is a fixed 7-player Werewolf game. Each game includes:

| role | count |
|---|---:|
| Wolf | 2 |
| Villager | 2 |
| Prophet | 1 |
| Guard | 1 |
| Witch | 1 |

Each run proceeds through role assignment, night actions, day discussion, voting, execution, deaths, and final win-condition judgment. Runs are controlled by seed and condition. The final public report uses STEP7-M RT2.3 N=20 as the main result.

The representative demo replay uses seed 58 from the scaffold arm. It is an explanatory case, not a substitute for the aggregate N=20 comparison.

## 4. Conditions

The final comparison uses two conditions:

| condition | description |
|---|---|
| `full-step7m-rt23` | Baseline full architecture without the final village scaffold |
| `full-village-scaffold-step7m-rt23` | Village-side scaffold with utterance, opinion, beneficiary, and interrogation cues |

The scaffold does not reveal hidden roles or correct answers. It changes the reasoning frame available to village-side agents by encouraging structured attention to public evidence.

## 5. Scaffold Design

The final scaffold is organized into four axes:

| axis | Korean label | intended observable effect |
|---|---|---|
| utterance analysis | 발화 분석 | More attention to contradiction, evasion, overconfidence, and inconsistency |
| public-opinion analysis | 여론 분석 | More tracking of accusation flow, bandwagon pressure, and scapegoating |
| beneficiary analysis | 수혜자 분석 | More explicit checks on who benefits from a vote or accusation shift |
| interrogation strategy | 심문 전략 | More follow-up questions, delayed judgment, and targeted pressure |

These axes are used as an experimental scaffold and as demo labels. They should not be interpreted as direct proof of internal cognition. The safe claim is that the scaffold changes the observable reasoning structure in logs and replay data.

## 6. Metrics

The project separates raw game-performance metrics from output-quality metrics.

| metric | source | interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Village win rate | raw game result | Whether the village team won |
| Role inference | raw vote/execution result | Whether executed targets were actually wolves |
| Vote consistency | raw individual votes | Whether individual votes pointed toward wolves |
| Average duration | raw game flow | How many days the game continued |
| Raw quality pass | quality harness | Whether original runs passed output hygiene checks |
| Replacements | sanitized display layer | Whether public display text required replacement |

This separation is important because sanitized display data can improve presentation hygiene without proving that the original agent reasoned better. In this project, raw performance metrics are not recalculated from sanitized text.

## 7. Results

### 7.1 Final RT2.3 N=20 Result

| arm | N | village win rate | wolf win rate | role inference mean±SD | vote consistency mean±SD | avg. duration mean±SD | raw quality pass | replacements |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| `full-step7m-rt23` | 20 | 0.200 | 0.800 | 0.317±0.404 | 0.334±0.257 | 2.000±0.649 | 20/20 | 0 |
| `full-village-scaffold-step7m-rt23` | 20 | 0.300 | 0.700 | 0.383±0.383 | 0.353±0.216 | 2.150±0.671 | 20/20 | 0 |
| delta | - | +0.100 | -0.100 | +0.066 | +0.019 | +0.150 | +0 | +0 |

The scaffold arm improved in the positive direction across all reported game metrics while preserving output-quality pass 20/20 and replacements 0. However, the effect sizes are small and the role-inference delta does not reach the pre-defined +0.10 strong-claim gate.

### 7.2 Interpretation

The result supports a cautious statement:

> Under output-quality control, the village scaffold produced exploratory positive trends in observable Werewolf behavior.

It does not support these stronger statements:

- The scaffold statistically significantly improves LLM NPC reasoning.
- The scaffold proves human-level social reasoning.
- The representative seed proves the aggregate result.
- Sanitized display quality proves raw reasoning quality.

In the same seed, the baseline condition executed a wolf first but later executed the prophet, producing a wolf victory. The scaffold condition executed wolves consecutively and produced a village victory. This case is useful for explaining what the scaffold changes in a replay interface, but the research claim remains tied to the N=20 aggregate table.

## 9. Discussion

The main value of the project is not that N=20 proves a stable improvement. It does not. The value is that the harness makes several confounds visible:

1. Surface fluency and game behavior can diverge.
2. Output hygiene must be separated from raw performance.
3. A scaffold can create positive directional movement while still failing a strong-claim gate.
4. Representative replays are useful for communication but must not replace aggregate metrics.

This framing is especially important for public demos. A single dramatic game can mislead viewers into believing the model is more robust than the repeated-run result supports. The final public package therefore keeps the demo, report, result table, and claim gate aligned.

## 10. Limitations

The current result has several limitations.

First, N=20 per arm is exploratory. It is enough to inspect trends and failure modes, but not enough for a strong statistical claim.

Second, the final public result uses one model family and one main game configuration. Additional models, model sizes, decoding settings, and game-rule variants are required before generalizing.

Third, role inference and vote consistency are rule-based proxies. They are useful because they are auditable, but they do not capture every aspect of social reasoning.

Fourth, the scaffold is currently evaluated at the aggregate behavior level. A stronger paper would add independent qualitative labels, error taxonomies, and per-axis event counts.

Fifth, the public export does not bundle the original raw run archives. This protects internal working context, but it also limits independent reproduction from the public repository alone.

## 11. Future Work

The next research steps are:

1. Increase N with the same seed-controlled harness.
2. Repeat the experiment across additional LLMs and model sizes.
3. Add independent human labeling for contradiction detection, scapegoating, and follow-up quality.
4. Report per-axis scaffold activation counts in addition to final game metrics.
5. Compare scaffold variants to test whether specific axes contribute differently.
6. Publish a reproducibility package with public-safe raw examples or synthetic equivalents.

## 12. Conclusion

This draft presents a controlled Werewolf harness for evaluating LLM NPC social reasoning. The final RT2.3 N=20 result shows small positive deltas for the village scaffold while preserving strict output-quality control. The result is not yet a strong performance proof. It is a disciplined exploratory result that shows how interdisciplinary social-reasoning scaffolds can be tested, bounded, and communicated without confusing polished dialogue with measured reasoning.

## Appendix A. Claim Gate

Allowed claims:

- The project implements a seed-controlled Werewolf harness for LLM NPC social-reasoning evaluation.
- The final RT2.3 N=20 scaffold arm shows positive but small deltas under output-quality control.
- The demo replay illustrates one representative scaffold win case.
- Raw performance metrics and sanitized display quality are separated.

Forbidden claims:

- The scaffold proves statistically significant improvement.
- The system achieves human-level or AGI-level social reasoning.
- The representative seed proves the aggregate result.
- Sanitized dialogue quality proves raw reasoning quality.
- The result generalizes across all LLMs or all social deduction settings.
